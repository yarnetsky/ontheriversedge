09.30.03

Long tired days

Long tired days

The days after a big show are always a struggle. Darlene and I have napped several hours in the past two days. Maybe tomorrow will feel normal. Maybe tomorrow we'll get something accomplished (I don't count paying bills as accomplishing anything ;)

The photo I popped up on top is one of the many barges I see floating by daily. I kind of wish they actually stopped here. On occasion, they send a row boat to shore to grab some supplies, but that's about it. The river industry that made this town thrive is just gone.

09.29.03

Chautauqua Report, Day 2

Chautauqua Report, Day 2

*Whew* Finally.

We have not had a decent sales at an arts festival since 9/11/01. This was the first.

Saturday was OK, but Sunday was better--surprisingly so as the crowds were way down from the opening day.

Darlene sold another three sculptures Sunday including one to a couple where the better (or worse) half wants to buy another sculpture as a surprise Christmas gift.

It might have been even better had a storm not shown up at 3:30 p.m.

About two minutes before the clouds let loose, I walked into the street in front of our booth and saw a curtain of rain coming up the valley. My jaw dropped, I have never seen rain come right at me like that.

Everyone was yelling warnings about the rain. Customers were running down the street then, also seeing the rain, turned tail and ran the other way.

Fortunately, a few friends showed up at that exact moment and helped us lay the pots into boxes so winds wouldn't blow them down.

It hit, but fortunately, the storm wasn't as bad as it looked.

Today we're depositing the money and quickly divying the proceeds among the various utility companies (as long as we don't confuse our Verizon and Vectren bills we'll be in good shape).

Then we get to unpack all the pots and try to set up our studio display again.

09.28.03

Chautauqua report, day one

Chautauqua report, day one

After being worried about getting rained out all day Saturday, we woke up in the morning to happily find it had blown through overnight.

The afternoon was sunny, warm and breezy. The wind pushing diamond tipped waves up river.

Our new booth space on the riverfront may not be as good sales-wise as our old locations, but it is definitely more picturesque.

We spent the day watching the river and the Kentucky shoreline. Boats from up and down river pulled ashore. A few barges loaded with coal and some sort of gaseous cargo plowed upriver, the wake rocking everything.

The trees in the valley and climbing up the Kentucky bluffs were golden-tipped, but not yet ready to give up its green to autumn.

Behind us was the Lanier Mansion in butterscotch and merrangue coloring with it's very nice gardens and greens. We sat on the 160-year-old stone fence that surrounded the grounds.

Sales were better than last month. People weren't as hesitant to buy the big pieces, Darlene sold a sculpture and several large bowls. However, we won't know if overall sales are holding up until after today.

We got another load out of the kiln and are packing to head out.

09.26.03

Madison Chautauqua Festival of Art

Madison Chautauqua Festival of Art

The website's not that great, but the weekend is. More than 200 artists attract 50,000 to 70,000 people to town over the weekend.

The show doesn't start until tomorrow, but already the streets are filling up. I hesitate to move the van until I absolutely have to for fear of losing the space until Sunday night.

Every morning, I roll my bike out the front door on my way to work and whip around the corner as I mount. Since I bought my bike in mid-July, I have never seen anyone as I round the corner. This morning of course I nearly hit an elderly couple. CRINGE. I need to remember these things.

In general, the show is really nice and features good art. However, compared to a few years ago, the quality of work is going down. It used to be gallery quality, but they have let in more country crafts. Not bad stuff, just not the stuff that will attract the upper-end clientel our town could thrive off of.

Sometimes I just don't think the people who live here "get it" and see what a gem this place is. They're still in the mode of cheap antique shoppers. We can attract a lot better clientel than that. Indeed, we need to in order to keep the businesses on Main Street healthy in the long run.

It's hurting right now because tourism is down and the money to market tourism comes from tourism (hotel taxes) so the situation could just get worse.

The flip of this equation is that people from Chicago, Key West and LA are discovering us and buying up property. We're not paying attention to this trend either. We need to insure a means of keeping affordable housing, espcially in the downtown historic district.

It's all a balance.

One woman from Bloomington bought a small little brick house. More cottage than house really. She calls it her "menopause home." She paid more than $70,000 for it. For as small as the place was, that seems really high to me. Mind you the location was awesome, just across from the Lanier Mansion, and the condition was decent.

I guess the kicker is that I make "Madison" wages and can't afford it. She make "Bloomington" wages and can. Expensive here is cheap elsewhere.

Again, it's all a balance.

OK, I'm off my soapbox for the afternoon.
