01.25.04

Up in a plume of flame and a poof of smoke

our microwave died today. May it's 2.6ghz soul rest in peace.

It's not surprising really as it hadn't been working well lately. However, its last symptoms were bizarre indeed.

We have a florescent light in our living/bedroom that hasn't worked properly for about six months now. This morning it was on and everything else in the apartment was barely running.

Our lights and our plugs are on two different circuits so it made no sense to me why when a light kicks on that our humidifier would kick off, but it did. We turned off the lights and the plugs would die. Turn on the lights and plugs come alive. These puppies are on two different circuits!

So we call our friend who's working on our electricity and he showed up within an hour.

After some messing around with the wiring we heard a huge pop and saw our microwave catch on fire with flames shooting out the bottom.

Fortunately, Darlene was right there and was able to cut the juice and the whole thing died in a huge poof of smoke. From that point on, everything was working in its normal fashion again.

As close as we can figure, the people who originally installed the electric put two hot wires and only one neutral in the same conduit. The surge from the microwave somehow bridged the two circuits.

Hmm, I wonder how much microwaves cost. We haven't been in the market for one for a while as we've had this one nearly as long as we've been married.

In the past 20 years, the Republicans have brilliantly developed the method "framing" issues to steer voters in their favor. It's not much different from a reporter asking a "leading question." You bias a question with the intent of getting a specific answer.

"Cool! I want to go tomorrow!"-- Ben, age 5

They were clumped together by the dozen, hanging from the ceiling hibernating.

Darlene and I went with our friends Caryl and Laura and Laura's two sons to Clifty Falls State Park this afternoon in the sultry 22 degree weather for a hike.

There's a century old railroad tunnel at the park. A guy, who was later Governor of Ohio, tried to build a railroad through Madison, but went bankrupt before he managed to get the thing finished. "Brough's Folly" has left behind a few interesting artifacts including the incomplete tunnel through the canyon bluffs of Clifty Creek.

The entrance has several underground creeks running nearby so the ceiling of the tunnel was covered with icicle stalactites.

Somehow we managed to convince the two boys, the oldest of whom is 5, to go through the tunnel. They were a bit scared at first, but I encouraged them by showing them all the fun things in there.

I held Ben's hand while Caryl took younger Alex and we walked them through over the rocks that have fallen over the decades and trying to avoid the standing water on the bottom of the tunnel.

At one place, the whole ceiling fell out and with our good flashlight you could see a couple dozen feet into the hole above us.

Then there's the underground creek that carved a crevice in the ceiling. There was a waterfall there today and the wall was encrusted with iron deposits. That was cool, but showing them the big spiders in the crevice evenly spaced every six inches was "Cool!!"

Ben wanted to turn back, but I encouraged him to go just a little further so he could see my favorite part of the tunnel in the winter. He grudingly agreed.

Fortunately, soon we were able to see either side of the tunnel and that encouraged him.

We got about halfway when we found them, probably 100 or so bats hibernating in clumps of 4 to 40 bats each.

Ben's first reaction was "aargh! bats!" But I shined my light at them and showed him how they were furry with cat-like ears. "Aren't they cute Ben?" "Awww, yeah..."

He then took the flashlight and searched the ceiling and walls for more. "There's some and there some."

Boy, it's fun when you can get a kid whose scared of something and turn them into a big fan. "Boy this is fun!" I helped him climb over huge boulders on the other side. He was a real trooper.

Their mom is due with the third brother in April, so we promised that we'd take the boys playing some more this spring.

Bush's approval rating drops from 60% to 50% - Immigration proposal falls flat†

The latest CBS News/NY Times poll has Bush taking a big drop in the latest approval polls to equal the lowest of his administration.

On one hand the constant polling of America drive me crazy because it makes it too easy for reporters to write "horse race" stories during an election year.

However, I find it interesting that his two latest "think big" initiatives are falling flat.

The proposed immigration policy appears to have hit a raw nerve. Several of our more conservative friends are mad as hornets over the proposal. Unfortunately I didn't get to talk to them long enough to find out why.

If the economy hadn't dumped 3 million jobs, I think people would be more open to his proposal. Even if the competition for jobs is more imagined than real, people are feeling defensive.

Nonetheless, I agree the United States needs to rethink it's immigration policies. On a simple human rights basis, it's sad that people subject themselves to near death experiences to make it here.

Also, as much as I hate to say it, I agree with Bush that there are jobs people no longer want to take.

Why are there jobs Americans don't want? I think there are several elements at work.

First off, we're consuming more and more and wanting more and more services.

"Hello, welcome to Taco Bell, would you like to try our new bean, cheese and rice burrito today?"
"No thank you, I'll take a 7-Layer Burrito please."
"Would you like hot or mild sauce with that?
"No thank you"
"That'll be $1.73, please pull around"

In this scenario, the kid on the other side of the microphone knows there's 10 more jobs just like this one waiting for him if he wants to quit tomorrow. A couple of our friends who run restaurants have learned this one the hard way.

Then there is Walmart.

Walmart is getting us great prices. I admit that I find it hard to pay $4 for a box of cereal at Krogers when I know I can get the same box for $2.75 at Wally World. I just don't make enough to pay $4 for cereal.

Yet, there is a flip side to this. To give us great prices, Walmart is pushing down wages everywhere else. Not only what it pays its own employees, but also what it's willing to pay suppliers for goods.

The suppliers in turn, to make a profit, can't pay its employees all that much. For agriculture, this means immigrants. For manufacturing, it means going overseas.

Because neither the service economy jobs, nor the Walmart jobs pay diddly, people are less willing to take them -- unless they don't have the option.

Unfortunately, we also know a lot people who don't have the option to turn down Walmart jobs as Madison does not have many well paying jobs either.

Again, if we hadn't lost so many jobs in the past couple of years, people wouldn't even think about these jobs. But today, I think they are.

But then there are the minority who believe they're the only true Americans and that immigration should be cut. I won't tell them that they're only the fourth-generation to live on our soil if you won't.

Personally, I like immigration. It keeps this country vibrant. We're getting a decent number of Latinos and Asians coming into town and I think it's exciting that our pleasent, though white bread, community is getting a little diversity.

Letter to Congressman Baron Hill†

Dear Sir,

Our nation's space program definitely needs a new set of priorities to succeed in the 21st century. A shuttle replacement is a decade overdue and ISS has been a funding black hole.

But cutting Hubbel in the process toward a manned trip to Mars grates my nerves. The Hubbel has done nothing short of revolutionizing astronomy and we should endeavor to keep it going until the new Webb telescope can take its place beside it. Combining the power of the two telescopes could exponentially increase the data.

This alone would give us a much better understanding of our universe than a trip to Mars.

You need to keep planning for manned space flight, but if Bush is only going to give NASA another $1 billion over five years, we'd get a lot more science out of robots. Instead we will be short changing every mission to gain enough money to get back to the moon. I can't imagine constructing a moon base would be cheaper then Alpha. I'd rather be visiting comets, asteroids and the planets.

Thus I'm skeptical of the administration's motivations. When the president shoots for the moon, but gives you a budget for a vacation, something's wrong.

sincerely,

E.G. Yarnetsky
Madison, Indiana

Unfortunately, I can't find the lens. I just don't remember it falling out. I fear it happened while we were on our walk this afternoon and the lens is out on the street somewhere.

Worst case scenario I'm without my glasses until next week. I can order a replacement on Monday, but I may not get it for several days.

Thus I think I'll be the opposite of "bright-eyed and bushy-tailed" for a few days.

01.06.04

534,000 cubic feet per second and -10F

Boy is it cold tonight. We're looking to bottom out at 4F with wind chill of -10F. Darlene and I pledged to walk after work and we only got down to the river and back (four blocks).

As frigid as it was, it was worth the walk, because our river is moving and fast. All the rain and snow that socked the Northeast and Midwest is flowing past the foot of our street.

According to the National Weather Service the Ohio River is flowing by our street at a rate of 534,000 cubic feet per second. The river is wild and as wide as a lake.

The river depth this morning was observed at 44.4 ft and was forecast to peak at 45.2 feet. Observing the line of grunge left behind, it looked as if it had just peaked when we were down there this evening.

Another foot or so and it would have covered some low lying roads. The river gets this high at least once every other year.

Normality? Normal depth around 20 feet and the historic average flow for January is 147,000 ft3/s.

Meanwhile, our apartment is staying moderately comfortable. We are wearing sweat jackets, but at least we're not cold. Putting plastic over our windows is paying off. The studio however is freezing between heating cycles.

Happy Happy Joy Joy

Boy that was quick!

I got home for lunch today to find Darlene laid out on our bed playing with our computer. Our computer! It came back!

Just before lunch Airborne Express dropped off the box with our Apple iBook enclosed. We had just dropped the puppy off at the Cincinnati Apple Store on Friday. They said the turnaround averaged 5-7 days. We're used to people meaning business days so we didn't expect it back until the end of the week at the earliest.

As requested they repaired my modem, power plug and the screen is looking as good as ever! I can open it again without it flicking off! To do all this I know they had to break it into pieces.

Better yet, they not only repaired those three things, but they also did some nice additional work as well.

The period key that broke off my keyboard? Fixed.

The keys my oily, speed-typing fingers rubbed clean?
Replaced.

The little rubber feet on the bottom of the laptop that dropped off two years ago? Replaced.

The trackpad and button corroded by two-and-a-half years of use in a pottery? REPLACED with new! Indeed the whole top panel is brand new.

This computer hasn't looked this good since Jan. 2002.

I should have remembered about the little right speaker being out. I knew I forgot something. But, hey I feel like I got my $300 worth. My computer works again and should last me another year at least.

"Let the surfing begin!" Darlene declared.
