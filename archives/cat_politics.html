05.16.05

A new morality for the 21st century?

For my library management class I'm reading an interesting book called "Civic Librarianship" by Ronald B. McCabe.

McCabe argues that the libertarian trends of the 60s-mid 90s created a nation where communities and civic institutions have been reduced to business models with no moral authority to help society. Libraries, schools and newspapers, for examples, are no longer educators and protectors of our civic society, but simple purveyors of information.

This in turn causes management and the public to devalue the civic professionals such as teachers, librarians and journalists. The library world is screaming at these trends--Philadelphia, for example, has been planning to reduce numerous branches to "express libraries" staffed only with paraprofessionals. The reasoning? Who needs professional librarians in a world where libraries are becoming nothing more than glorified bookstores?

McCabe argues this trend started in the 1960s when counter-culture rebeled against the "moral" authority of authority. In the 1980s, the right joined in on the fun and tossed in an economic libertariansim that not only eliminated much of the moral checks and balances of business, but in turn forced civic institutions to follow business-oriented models of operation.

I'm just under half way through the book so I haven't read too many of his "solutions," but it revolves around the notion that society needs to regain it's shared "morality" that emphasizes a balance between rights and responsiblities-- both socially and economic.

I admit that when it comes to talk of "morality," my fears ask the question "whose morality?" Nonetheless, I think he is correct.

He uses another author's analogy of society as a mosiac. The pieces are all individual and that individuality is not lost. However, there is a frame and a glue that keep the pieces together and creates something bigger than the pieces. The glue is "shared" morality and the frame is community.

This all makes good sense, but the key is obviously the word "shared." While there are indeed morals the supermajority of the public could agree on, we are now in a climate where some of the shards would rather cut us than become a part of the mosiac.

I'll follow up when I read more of the book. I'm curious how the "community movement" could equal a new "civic librarianship."

I'd like to hear more on possible solutions as it's obvious something needs to happen, but so far I see two problems with this solution.

First, in order to liberate the younger folks' money into these accounts, the government would have to borrow tanker loads of money. The problem is who will pay this debt? I'd rather be visiting comets, asteroids and the planets.

    Thus I'm skeptical of the administration's motivations. When the president shoots for the moon, but gives you a budget for a vacation, something's wrong.
