04.23.05

Weird April weather

Driving home from Indianapolis after classes last night was a real experience.

The sky turned green and I can't even begin to describe what the clouds looked like. Sufice it to say that they weren't the usual flat bottomed cumulonimbus.

A minute later all hell broke loose with wind and rain so hard I couldn't see out my windshield. I pulled over and watched the hail bounce all over the place.

I called Darlene on my cell and she told me there were tornado warnings to my east and north. No surprise there.

It finally slowed and I drove home. I passed a ton of cars hiding under overpasses which I could have sworn I heard was the worst thing to do.

About a 40 minutes later the western sky cleared and the setting sun illuminated the treetops. It was unreal. The tree bottoms were still shadowed by the storm clouds, but a sharp line of bright (and I mean bright) green leaves contrasted against a still inky sky. I drove bugeyed through the light rain watching the scene when suddenly a bright double rainbow highlighted the road infront of me. Of course, the rainbow (and the road) ended in Madison.

This morning my father-in-law called. The same storm system is now hitting them and they're expecting 4 inches of snow. Unfortunately, all their fruit trees are in blossom and they're going to lose all the pre-started corn they planted just last week.

March came in like a lion and out like a lamb. April came in like a lamb and is leaving like an angry ram.

01.08.05

Waters are a coming?

Well, I guess the Midwest can't get a snow storm and then days of rain without the Ohio River sending it all back our way.

The National Weather Service's Advanced Hydrologic Prediction Service is saying the Ohio River will likely top flood stage here in Madison Sunday morning. Here's the graph...

I covered the last flood in 1997 extensively for The Madison Courier. I'm hoping there won't be a repeat. Houses along Vaughn Drive (our river road) were flooded halfway up the first floor. The river hit 460.90 feet, the eighth worst recorded flood at Madison.

It was amazingly scary to look inside the homes after being visited by the Ohio River. A half foot of river silt covered every surface from the floor to the sinks. The walls were laid bare half-way to the ceiling, the drywall disintegrated. We are so used to seeing the barriers, suddenly seeing straight through a house with only beams in the way is like looking at a ghost.

Note: The river crests are measured from sea level, not the bottom of the river. For comparison, the river bottom at Madison is at 403.2 or 402.3 (I can never remember which). It's normal depth at this time of year would probably be about 420. I added the river stage under Madison links.

12.29.04

The big melt

Madison, Indiana, the most beautiful slush city in America.

The "official" tally was 18-inches of snow in 24-hours. The total landed our county on the state's emergency disaster aid list in the state. That might help pay the overtime of our diehard snow guys. They did an awesome job even working through Christmas, though they did take turns going home for periods to be with family.

That subsequent freeze caused havoc on our town's guttering. Buildings all across downtown had huge icicles hanging from the roof as the downspouts froze over.

Across the street this morning, absolutely huge gutter-shaped chunks of ice came raining down on the sidewalk as the guttering collapsed.

Another building had their windows cracked by cascading ice.

At our library, a frozen downspout caused the melting snow and ice water to run down the side of the building and flooding into the children's room. We got the books off the shelf largely in time, though we lost maybe 8 books. When I pulled one of the wet books off the shelf, it was entitled-- "I Got Wet."

Our snow from Darlene's perspective

Editor's note: This is an edited version of what Darlene wrote my mom. It stated our situation so perfectly that I got her permission to post it here. Here's Darlene...

Imagine our downtown where many of the residents do not have off street parking. Now imagine 17 inches of snow dumped on that town in less than 48 hours...

Jer and I returned this afternoon and I parked the van in the space we dug it out of. As soon as I pulled all the way in, it occurred to me that getting back out could be a challenge. Said parking space is still snow covered, a bit slippery, and the nose of the van is downhill a bit from the rear of the van.

Out of sheer stubborness I decided I was going to try to get van out myself and pick Jerry up at 5. Felt I should have practice in doing this sort of thing as you never know when you might need the skills. Proceeded outside.

Cardboard under wheels- no luck. Moving van back and forth, no luck. (only proceeded in moving to the far right side of the parking space) Hopped out of car to reposition cardboard. Landed in 3 foot drift, slid out of drift to bounce on my kneecaps on the street. (3 points for humoring any onlookers at gas station). Repositioned cardboard, trudged back through snowdrift and tried again. It is about 22 degrees outside and 2 kind boys on bikes volunteered to push. They were not very large, but quite insistent. (a bit bored I suspect) No luck. Man pulls up in truck, attaches chain to van and with boys pushing on other end I am freed!

Slowly make my way across slush covered streets to library where I happily drop the problem in Jerry's lap. Ok hon, if we park in our space, we may not be able to get back out again. Other spaces either have 4 foot drifts in them or cars covered in four foot drifts in them. City lots are still snow covered.

Head to nearest city parking lot. Maybe it was more usable than it appeared. Just before pulling in ran into friend Ellen who had just her own car freed from the snow. Jerry proceeds cautiously into lot not realizing where Ellen had just been freed from! Immediately get stuck in same mess that Ellen had just been pulled from in said parking lot. Ellen's husband and father in law and another friendly bystander along with Jerry proceed to push us out of drift and some kind (and probably exasperated) soul with a front loader plows a path to allow us to get out of the lot.

So with no other parking opportunities, we were forced back to our same sloping parking space that we dug ourselves out of Thursday and will probably have to be pushed out of Tuesday afternoon.

This is how we are managing the snow here in Madison. ( I am really glad we are not in Texas or Louisiana!) I can't wait to see what happens when businesses and the courthouse, city hall etc. reopen tomorrow.

I asked for snow and so far I am still amused by it.

05.27.04

Even cicadas like our library

I've heard people call Brood X an invasion. The cicadas are definitely in full force here in Madison and we are inundated with the red-eyed, vainy-winged bugs.

As the front door of our library opens, the buzz roars in and fades out as the door shuts once again.

That's been the scenario for the past 10 days. Today, however, I think the cicadas have finally realized what a cool library we have. They're riding their way in on the backs of our patrons.

This afternoon, a mom was checking out books as I was helping her daughter learn how to use Indiana's Virtual Library Inspire.net. Suddenly, we heard a very loud chirping from across the reading room. The young woman's mother was jumping around clearly stunned. Soon, the poor woman realized there was a cicada in her purse.

Not three minutes later, I'm still helping the student find journal articles on viruses and an older gentleman walked in and started chirping as well. As he found one in his pocket, the two of us couldn't keep from laughing. He promptly dropped it on the floor and crunched it with his foot. Just what we needed, crushed cicada on our carpet.

An hour later, I was checking out some videos for a young man who suddenly grabbed at his neck and found a cicada crawling down the collar of his shirt. Even knowing he had it in his hand we both jumped when the thing started chirping. Those puppies are loud.

The buggers are falling from the trees, swarm at the lightest distraction and, unlike flies, know nothing of people, dogs, cars or anything else on earth for that matter. They're all over the sidewalks and streets, many dead having been stomped on or driven over. I had a couple "riding" on my bike.

They're even showing public displays of affection. I now regularly see pairs of cicadas on top of each. I was suddenly glad not to have children at this immediate point. Not only would I have to explain the birds and the bees, but cicadas as well.

One of our patrons in this very boat said she was very happy when her kid readily settled for the answer of "they're mating." She's not sure if the child knew what the word meant, but was happy to not have to explain it.

While I'm glad we've visited by Brood X only once every 17 years, I'm enjoying the spectacle.

This morning I went to the Hanover College library to pick up some inter-library loan books that were mis-routed seven miles in the wrong direction. Hanover is a very leafy campus and it was evident from the holes in the ground that there were a few million bugs along with the 2,000 students.

The funny thing was the sound. There were so many of them that the overall aural feel of the cicada calls had changed. Instead of sounding like crickets on acid, the hum took on a whiring sound not unlike what you'd hear from space ships in 1950s sci-fi movies.

Hmmm, maybe we are being invaded.

04.14.04

Snow in mid-April???

You have to understand that this is an area where we get dafodils and tulips in mid-March. Winter end there.

But on Tuesday it snowed, covering blooming dogwoods and redbuds with heavy, slushy snow.

While I was attending the Indiana Library Federation annual conference in Indianapolis, an odd front moved through that dumped 2-6 inches of really wet, heavy snow along the Ohio River valley upriver from Louisville.

My boss and I were driving down I-65 when it started raining hard as we left Indy. Sometime after we passed Columbus, Ind., Charlene asked "what is that stuff." The white stuff was only in patches, but as we drove further south it piled up.

By the time we got off the Interstate onto IN-256 it was obvious we had a mid-spring snow storm. Cars and trucks were spun off the road. Soon we caught up to the snow plow that sent plumes of snow (and several trash cans) 20 feet into the air.

The bushes were heavy with snow, green leaves and flowers pokes out from the snow. It was quite beautiful.

I wish I had actually witnessed the event unstead of the after effects.

Darlene said it started with rain and turned to a heavy sleet. Soon silver dollar-sized flakes fell in the heaviest snow seen the whole year.

Darlene said her favorite, though sad episode of the snow was a police car who flew down Main Street toward a wreck on the west end of town. As he crossed the railroad bridge, he hit black ice, spun out and hit a car. That car hit another car and that car hit a house.

The next morning I biked to work, the snow was already gone a mid-spring night's dream.

Where this Egyptian Goose came from is anyone's guess. There's good odds it has escaped from a zoo or private collection.

Maybe he's slowly working its way down the Ohio to Cairo, Ill., thinking its heading to Cairo, Egypt, on the Nile.

01.19.04

"Cool! I want to go tomorrow!"-- Ben, age 5

They were clumped together by the dozen, hanging from the ceiling hibernating.

Darlene and I went with our friends Caryl and Laura and Laura's two sons to Clifty Falls State Park this afternoon in the sultry 22 degree weather for a hike.

There's a century old railroad tunnel at the park. A guy, who was later Governor of Ohio, tried to build a railroad through Madison, but went bankrupt before he managed to get the thing finished. "Brough's Folly" has left behind a few interesting artifacts including the incomplete tunnel through the canyon bluffs of Clifty Creek.

The entrance has several underground creeks running nearby so the ceiling of the tunnel was covered with icicle stalactites.

Somehow we managed to convince the two boys, the oldest of whom is 5, to go through the tunnel. They were a bit scared at first, but I encouraged them by showing them all the fun things in there.

I held Ben's hand while Caryl took younger Alex and we walked them through over the rocks that have fallen over the decades and trying to avoid the standing water on the bottom of the tunnel.

At one place, the whole ceiling fell out and with our good flashlight you could see a couple dozen feet into the hole above us.

Then there's the underground creek that carved a crevice in the ceiling. There was a waterfall there today and the wall was encrusted with iron deposits. That was cool, but showing them the big spiders in the crevice evenly spaced every six inches was "Cool!!"

Ben wanted to turn back, but I encouraged him to go just a little further so he could see my favorite part of the tunnel in the winter. He grudingly agreed.

Fortunately, soon we were able to see either side of the tunnel and that encouraged him.

We got about halfway when we found them, probably 100 or so bats hibernating in clumps of 4 to 40 bats each.

Ben's first reaction was "aargh! bats!" But I shined my light at them and showed him how they were furry with cat-like ears. "Aren't they cute Ben?" "Awww, yeah..."

He then took the flashlight and searched the ceiling and walls for more. "There's some and there some."

Boy, it's fun when you can get a kid whose scared of something and turn them into a big fan. "Boy this is fun!" I helped him climb over huge boulders on the other side. He was a real trooper.

Their mom is due with the third brother in April, so we promised that we'd take the boys playing some more this spring.

Whenever I think of bats (rated PG-13 parental discretion is advised).

I had a blast, but whenever I think of bats, I can't help to think about the book "Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas."

I've never read the book, but an old friend once did a radio show where he played "road trip music" and between tunes read from books about road trips. He read a passage where the main characters are having to cover some sort of event in Las Vegas, but en route decided to go to LA and buy huge quantities of drugs and take them all en route to LV. After a ungodly dose of ether, the protagonist (if you want to call him that) starts to hallucinate.

01.06.04

534,000 cubic feet per second and -10F

Boy is it cold tonight. We're looking to bottom out at 4F with wind chill of -10F. Darlene and I pledged to walk after work and we only got down to the river and back (four blocks).

As frigid as it was, it was worth the walk, because our river is moving and fast. All the rain and snow that socked the Northeast and Midwest is flowing past the foot of our street.

According to the National Weather Service the Ohio River is flowing by our street at a rate of 534,000 cubic feet per second. The river is wild and as wide as a lake.

The river depth this morning was observed at 44.4 ft and was forecast to peak at 45.2 feet. Observing the line of grunge left behind, it looked as if it had just peaked when we were down there this evening.

Another foot or so and it would have covered some low lying roads. The river gets this high at least once every other year.

Normality? Normal depth around 20 feet and the historic average flow for January is 147,000 ft3/s.

Meanwhile, our apartment is staying moderately comfortable. We are wearing sweat jackets, but at least we're not cold. Putting plastic over our windows is paying off. The studio however is freezing between heating cycles.

Things happen when the boats come. You can't buy a newspaper anywhere because the "boat people" snag them all. They come to the library to check their email. A number of them also stopped by the studio to watch Darlene throw pots. She said we had guests from Utah, Nevada and California.

03.25.02

Main Street is glossy

Main Street is glossy with a spring rain today. The town looks much nicer wet as our century old brick buildings are washed clean. The colors are simply richer as the clouds alternatively highlight and soften the 400-foot bluffs surrounding our town.

We've seen a lot of rain -- several inches in the past week. The Ohio River has swollen into a running lake nearly flooding Vaughn Drive. It's the first time the river has threatened to overflow its banks since the nasty flood in 1997. While I'm sure the dafodils love the new rain today, but the local humans are ready to dry out a bit. With flowers blooming downtown, we're being teased with spring. We want more. You bet we want more.
