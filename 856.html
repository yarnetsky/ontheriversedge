When Opportunity knocks

It sure is fun to find the NASA scientists with the jaws on the ground.

The second Mars rover “Opportunity” couldn’t have landed in a more interesting spot on Mars. Smack dab in a crater with a layer dusty grey hematite covering Martian bedrock. What caused the stuff? Volcanos? Water? Awesome stuff.

Seeing the first photo of the deep red stuff, Darlene, spoken like a true potter, couldn’t resist wanting some to glaze her pots with.

I loved the quote from Dr. Squyres in today’s NY Times.

To Dr. Steven W. Squyres, the dark, surreal landscape encircling the Mars rover Opportunity resembled only one other place.

“This is exactly what it looked like in my wildest dreams,” said Dr. Squyres, a professor of astronomy at Cornell University who is principal investigator for the two NASA rovers currently on Mars…..

Dr. Squyres said he had expected Opportunity, at best, to land close enough to reach a crater. “Instead, what has happened is that we have scored a 300-million-mile interplanetary hole in one,” he said Sunday afternoon. Opportunity, he said, appears to be inside a shallow crater about 20 yards wide and a couple of yards deep.

With a shot like that their handicap should go down quite a bit.

    We have a small fireworks show each Regatta week on the Ohio River. It’s nothing extravegant, but it’s fun. You can watch the reflections on the river, but we were on our roof a couple blocks away.
