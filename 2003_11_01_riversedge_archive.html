{11.27.2003}

Thanksgiving Day Football 

We had Thanksgiving Dinner at Darlene's younger sister's house tonight. Nearly the whole family showed up (about 30). I played made up games with the neices and nephews and ate plenty of good food. This year's home grown turkey came in at 41 pounds.

It's not a Thanksgiving, however, unless we get to play football.

We played what Devin (a high school football player) calls Farm Football-- Out in the pasture with cow patties to make things interesting (as well as plenty of puddles from today's constant rain). My team couldn't catch the ball, but we managed a good running game and won 3 to 2. The last two touchdowns came at the onset of darkness. We used the darkness to run sneaky plays-- they had no idea who had the ball until it was too late.

Mind you, the flavor of the game came from the two llamas at the 20 yard line and the cow in the end zone.

Happy Thanksgiving family and friends

{11.26.2003}

UNO 

It's 1 a.m. and we just got home from playing euchre and UNO with our friends for the last five hours. Boy what a wild time.

For those who play UNO, here's a great house rule we were introduced to tonight.

When a draw two or draw four is played, the next player can match it and so can the next. The player who can't match it is stuck with picking up the draws for all the matching cards.
Tonight we had four draw twos in a row so the fifth player had to pick up eight cards.

Darlene and I played draw fours so the next player picked up eight and then and I picked a color that forced the player after that to pick another eight cards because she couldn't match the color.

It's a pretty mean rule, but boy was it fun.

{11.22.2003}

Wild weekend 

The birthday party (prior post) capped a wild weekend. It was surprising considering the quiet week.

On the frustrating side, I've been working on a four-page 11x15 brochure for the local historic preservation organization. It's going to look rather nice. I'll post a pdf of it when I get a chance. However, the information I received from the printer the first time didn't quite match what they told me the second time. As a result I hit a brick wall.

The first time they simply gave me measurements, the second time they said they had a template they preferred I use-- they're not the same. Similarly, the first time I was supposed to convert the document to a pdf, the second time they said just send the original document. That also meant I needed to change the fonts I was using (in hind sight I could have guessed as much). Unfortunately, I did not have those fonts and had to bug a friend for copies to use on the project.

It's back on track, but I lost a weekend of time.

Meanwhile, our friend the tinsmith calls to have me bring by some of his work (we're selling his tinware from our studio) and two bottles of wine to his house ASAP.

He said he hit paydirt at match.com and found a date from Cincinnati-- a cute 47-year-old who believes in syncronicity. All week apparently she had been humming a certain song about the tinman from "The Wizard of Oz" and then meets a "tinman" on match.com.

They chatted online for four hours that night and he was smitten. She agreed to come down to Madison-- thus the wine.

He called the next day to say they sat up talking until 5:30 the next morning. That's all I know because our phone battery went dead. I'm sure I'll hear about it sometime.

Nice long hike. Having given up on reaching the tinman (he was probably off on his next phone call) we went out an hiked at Clifty Falls for a couple of hours. The park is so different after the leaves fall. The trails are trickier as the roots and rocks are covered in leaves. However, you can see all the way from the rock outcroppings down to Clifty Creek and to the far side of the canyon. The views are simply unavailable from March-October.

The effects of the rock concert are finally wearing off so maybe I can sleep.

My heart's still beating in 6/8 time 

We now know what an 18-year-old's birthday party is like.

Often adults get invited to such parties to keep the parents company, but we were invited by the 18-year-old himself who has kind of adopted us as honorary aunt and uncle (though the parents were glad to have company nonetheless).

We showed up late and missed most of the parlor games. Then again, that may have been a good thing. We did catch the tail end of one game where everyone kneels in a circle passing around a playing card... with their lips.
You hold the card up to your mouth and suck in air to hold the card to your lips and try to pass it to the next person who in turn tries to suck the card to their lips. I overheard the rules stipulate if you dropped the card you had to kiss that person. I did not, however, witness this in practice.

However, we did show up in time for the rock concert. Carl, the birthday boy, plays bass in a band with a few friends and they played a short set of pretty decent punk rock at full throttle in the living room. The floors vibrated as did my rib cage. The concert was capped by a Jimi Hendrix-style rendition of "Happy Birthday" with everyone screaming the lyrics.

Then there was the entertaining black and white cat. One guy was wearing pants about six inches to long and ripped at the foot. The cat would follow his feet and pounce on his pants. Later, Darlene took a breather from the concert to find the cat on the dining room table eating the birthday cheese cake.

Entertaining indeed.

death and taxes 

Everyone down here in the historic district is anxious as the date to pay property taxes is fast approaching. Our taxes went up about 30 percent, but many of our friends had their property taxes double and triple as a result of the state switching to "market value" property assessment.

The Indiana House Ways and Means Committee held hearings here in Madison yesterday to see how the change is impacting historic communities. A larger than usual number of properties are for sale down here as people are wanting to get out of the taxes.

As Benjamin Franklin said on this date in 1789-- "In this world nothing can be said to be certain, except death and taxes."

Almost got drafted 

I attended the annual meeting of the Indiana Visual-Audio Network today. I was appointed to it's overall board of directors by our library board (all member libraries get to appoint a member).

It's no big deal really. The only chore is to attend an annual meeting to brainstorm and rubberstamp some of the stuff the executive committee drafts such as the annual budget.

We had a nice lunch, I won an audio book as a door prize (darn-didn't get the DVD player) and did a tour of the brand new (and extremely impressive) Indianapolis Star printing plant.

During the brainstorming session I opened my mouth a couple of times and later when they were trying to fill a vacancy on the executive committee, one of the directors said "Hey Jerry, why don't you do it."

Would my director let me? will they pay me or is this volunteer? errrr---let me think about this. The past-president later pulled me aside to half-begged me to try it. I'll talk to my boss tomorrow. It'd be one day a month committment. It wouldn't be bad at all if I were within an hour of Indy, but four hours of driving for two hours of meeting is a bear. What's the phrase--make a suggestion and you volunteered?

For what it's worth IVAN is a state authorized library cooperative to circulate multimedia packages to member libraries (i.e.--we get a package of 40 videos for eight weeks and then ship them to the next library and get a fresh set of 40 videos. It's a nice for smaller libraries).

The day ended well 

The weather indeed got cooler, but it is still within the pleasent fall weather range so far. This weekend I may not say the same as it won't get out of the 40s.

For better or worse, I've come to somewhat dread Wednesday evenings in the children's room. I really enjoy helping the kids and parents find books and info, but I just don't get enough of it. Instead it's more like shelving books in the dungeon. It wouldn't be so bad if there was someone around, but it gets a little too quiet and lonely down there. I guess I'm too much of a people person.

The number of children's programs has been consistantly growing and with that the number of books flying out of the library is growing. Accordingly, the number of books to shelve gets larger as well. Actually, the library in general has been getting much busier, if the amount of shelving we're having to constantly do is an indication, our circulation is up. It'll be interesting to see the October stats that should be coming out any day now.

Either way, I got back to the library only to face two huge carts of books-- no I didn't get them all shelved. No one showed up to see the movie ("African Queen"- another movie I'd love to see on the big screen) and the children's room was again quiet.

I did get to show a mother how to set up a blog for her kid. She was hoping to get her son into a writing workshop we're holding, but one of the volunteers didn't show so we had to cut the registration off early. Instead, I suggested the son create a blog as it's certainly cooler than keeping a diary or journal. I figure getting any writing out of him would be a start. She liked the idea and will see how it goes.

That was the sole question on the night until Darlene surprised me at 8:40 and kept me company and helped me shelve books the last few minutes. We then slowly walked home in the autumn air and had a nice conversation punctuated by the kind of hugs on Main Street that has people calling us "those silly love birds." Maybe not, but you get the idea.

Incorrigible or procrastination? 

When websites first became hot. I realized that I'd be left behind if I didn't learn the code. I succeeded, but prefered using a program that would write the code for me--Freeway for Macintosh.

However, lately most of my clients have been wanting to update their sites more often than I have time to serve them. I guess that's the downside of taking too many clients when you're moonlighting.

Cascading Style Sheets are easy enough to understand on the face of it all, but I'm still trying to figure out why one combination of code works and another doesn't. It'd probably help if I just got a book. You wouldn't know I worked in a library.

Am I:

a) being an incorrible geek?

b) procrastinating on a paying print project that isn't working the way I want it to?

c) procrastinating relearning math, algebra and geometry in preparation for the Graduate Record Exam?

Owner's Manual. Human. Model 42 

This article contemplating an owner's manual for life is a little metaphysical for my tastes, but it hits on something I've often wondered. Wouldn't it be cool if everyone came with a user's manual? Then we'd have fewer disagreements and would be more fulfilled.

Maybe, maybe not. It would certainly undermine the enjoyment of learning about someone. Then again, I never read the instructions for a computer program unless I have to. But knowing it's there helps immensely.

Thus, if your fellow human is constantly bombing and giving you the "blue screen of death." Wouldn't it be nice to pull out their manual to help with the diagnostics and provide tips to avoid the situation in the future?

OK, mine might look like this...

Troubleshooting guide:
If-- human's main processor starts fuzzing out.
Sign of -- dehydration and/or lack of fuel to the brain.
Solution -- a glass of water and/or a little food.

Maintenance schedule:
Regular maintenance is required for this human. To insure continued optimal operation, do the following.
*Hugs at both regular and irregular intervals. Does not need excuses.
*Feed every 5 hours. Water every 2 hours.
*Take for road trip at least once every 21 rotations of the earth.

At a loss for thoughts-- my main processor is indeed fuzzy and the solution is on the burner --I asked Darlene what else would be in my manual.

She grinned.

"Rated G please."

"Okay, I guess that about covers it."

Hmm, I must be easy.
