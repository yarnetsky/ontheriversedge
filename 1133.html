Last night of freedom... classes start tomorrow

As you scroll down tonight, you’ll notice I’m in a bloggy mood. I don’t think I’ve written six posts in two days in the past eight months that I’ve been in grad school.

It think it is because tonight is my last night of freedom. My “Information Architecture for the Web” class starts tomorrow and “Information Sources and Services” class convenes on Saturday.

On one hand, these classes might be a little easier than my first four classes as I have some experience in both. Even so I need to come away from both classes with more advanced skills.

On the other hand, I’m just not ready to jump back into the fray.

When I talked to people about getting an MLS, they all said essentially the same thing—getting the degree is like running a marathon—you just have to keep going and until you cross the finish line you won’t have a life.

Mind you, I think the analogy faulters a bit when you take summer sessions. That’s like inserting a 400 meter dash between the marathons.

Yet, they told me it was doable. Now into my third semester, I’d have to agree. It’s doable and I’m doing it. But I’ll also admit to whining more than my fair share.

Oddly, I think the breaks are the worst. They let you breathe just long enough that when it comes time to get back into the groove you feel like you’re running the race in snowshoes—you just can’t get going again. Fortunately, deadlines are definite motivators.

All that said, I know I have little basis for whining. A friend of mine who I used to work with has six kids and is working on her undergraduate and plans on getting her graduate degree. She would have reason to whine. A classmate at IU is working on her MLS while having teens at home. She told me she has stayed up late many a night after running the kids around to their various practices and events her eyes turning to glaze at 11 p.m. She has reason to whine.

Me? Nah. I just have a wife who is constantly tempting me to blow of my homework and play (oops… that was my undergraduate years—we couldn’t wait to get married).
