Sometimes it’s hard being a geek…

Every day, I look through MacSurfer and gather all the news that’s fit to Mac. I’d have bought Apple stock at $8 a share if I had the money (boy do I wish). I own an iBook laptop and enjoys surfing in bed by Wi-Fi. Thus I might as well face it that I’m an Apple geek.

Being an Apple geek is easy. Steve Jobs comes out with fun products that work and you get to make fun of Microsoft too. (Though I’ll give Bill Gates tons of credit for giving computers to libraries).

No one harasses me for being an Apple geek. Indeed, people are sending people my way at the library to answer Mac questions. Being a geek can be fun.

Yet, as if it wasn’t obvious, I’m also among the 28 percent of Americans who are “closely following the election.” The extent to which would definitely classifies me as a political geek. For example, I don’t follow the national polls. I follow the state polls—the ones that really matter. Check out electoral-vote.com.

But being a political geek in a small town is hard sometimes.

On one hand, I’d love to go campaigning door-to-door for candidates who’ll make a difference. I did this once for a city council candidate in Toledo. You really feel like you make connections even if you lose (my candidate won state rep. the following year).

On the other hand, I don’t want my politics to interfer with my work or my wife’s pottery either.

I want everyone to feel comfortable they can come to me for help finding information. On the flip side, I don’t want to be harassed in the library for my views either.

Darlene and I already had a guy from Pennsylvania come into our pottery studio and literally bait us into a political argument.

By speaking out in public (or even putting a Kerry sign in my bedroom window), I fear I’d get more of the same or that my job would be compromised. Sometimes I feel even writing in this blog may cost me somehow in the future.

Yet, sometimes I feel like the call to speak out (and not just blog) overwhelming.

Let me give an example. There’s an article in this week’s Newsweek about how the Department of Homeland Security is seriously looking at getting the power from Congress to postpone the elections in case of a terrorist attack.

On the face of it, I find this scary. For the past year, my father-in-law kept saying Bush was going to try cancelling the election. But until he sent me a copy of the article, I just didn’t think anyone could think of altering such an integral part of our democracy. Now I’m not so sure. This is the same administration that’s pushing the Pakistani government to capture or kill top Al-Queda operatives during the first three days of the Democratic National Convention.

I’ll withhold judgement until I see the legislation, but I swear if I feel Bush is trying to corrupt the system, I’ll be protesting loud and long. At that point I won’t care about my reputation.
