Paying for the coronation

The more I read about it, the more the Bush coronation on Jan. 20 just gauls me. The fact $40 million dollars was spent on it is amazing—that’s a heck of a lot of political favors being bought.

Yet on the front pages of global newspapers, this extravagance contrasted poorly to photo of a little Iraqi girl splattered in her parents blood after her parents were killed by Americans at a check point. Is this a way to win hearts and minds? “Partying American’s kill little girls parents?” Here’s a Catholic nun’s take on the story.

[Mind you, from what I’ve heard so far the soldiers were in a damned if you do, damned if you don’t situation. When you have a vehicle that won’t stop for a checkpoint, even after warning shots, what do you do? Even shooting at the tires could give a driver time to explode the vehicle. Yet, more of this hell is sure to come. Proof in point? Hundreds of Iraqi police uniforms were stolen in Basra. Would you stop for anyone not knowing who the good guys were?]

Then I read that the local governments are being forced to pay $12 million for the inauguration’s extraordinary security. How are the locals supposed to pay for this unfunded federal mandate? The government is saying the locals should use money granted to them for homeland security. Money already earmarked for projects to make us safer. Hearing this do you think residents of Fairfax Co., Va., or Prince George’s Co., Md., are feeling safer now?

Yes, it’s arguable that Bush deserved to party, but couldn’t he have done it in a way that projects just a bit of compassion or maybe in a way that kept these homeland security fund going to projects that actually make those residents safer? Nah, that’s not their style.

    Fireworks in Madison

    We have a small fireworks show each Regatta week on the Ohio River. It’s nothing extravegant, but it’s fun. You can watch the reflections on the river, but we were on our roof a couple blocks away.

    My favorite thing is listening to the explosions reverberate forever off our 400-foot bluffs. This year’s finale was punctuated by dozens of such explosions which made for a very satisfying ending.
